# UNIT 1: Ecosystem & Environment

## 1. Atmosphere & Atmospheric Layers

**Definition:**
The blanket of gases surrounding the earth is called the atmosphere. It extends up to 500 km from the earth's surface and is held by gravity.

**Composition:**
*   **Nitrogen ($N_2$):** 78%
*   **Oxygen ($O_2$):** 21%
*   **Argon:** 0.9%
*   **Others ($CO_2$, water vapor, etc.):** 0.1%

**Structure of Atmosphere (Layers):**

> **Mnemonic:** **T**rust **S**tate **M**inisters **Th**en **Ex**ecute
> (Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere, Exosphere)

1.  **Troposphere (0-12 km):**
    *   Lowest layer, where humans live.
    *   All weather phenomena (rain, cloud, storm) happen here.
    *   Temperature **decreases** with altitude (Lapse Rate).

2.  **Stratosphere (12-50 km):**
    *   Contains the **Ozone Layer** ($O_3$).
    *   Absorbs harmful UV radiation.
    *   Stable air; ideal for **Jet Aircrafts**.
    *   Temperature **increases** with altitude.

3.  **Mesosphere (50-80 km):**
    *   Coldest layer (-100°C).
    *   Meteors burn up here upon entering.

4.  **Thermosphere (80-700 km):**
    *   Hottest layer (up to 2000°C) due to solar radiation.
    *   Contains **Ionosphere** (helps in radio communication).
    *   Aurora Borealis (Northern Lights) occurs here.

5.  **Exosphere (>700 km):**
    *   Outermost layer, fades into space.
    *   Satellites orbit here.

## 2. Ecological Pyramid

**Definition:**
A graphical representation of the relationship between different organisms in an ecosystem at different trophic levels.

**Key Concepts:**
*   **Producer:** Base of the pyramid (Plants).
*   **Consumer:** Primary (Herbivores), Secondary (Carnivores), Tertiary (Top Predators).
*   **Energy Flow:** Unidirectional (10% Law - only 10% energy moves to the next level).

**Types of Pyramids:**

1.  **Pyramid of Number:**
    *   Represents the *number* of individuals at each level.
    *   **Upright:** Grassland (Grass > Deer > Lion).
    *   **Inverted:** Parasitic (Tree < Birds < Parasites).

2.  **Pyramid of Biomass:**
    *   Represents the total dry *weight* of organisms.
    *   **Upright:** Forest ecosystem.
    *   **Inverted:** Pond ecosystem (Phytoplankton/Algae have less mass than Whales/Big Fish).

3.  **Pyramid of Energy:**
    *   Represents energy flow.
    *   **ALWAYS UPRIGHT.** Energy always decreases as we move up (Lindeman's 10% Law).

## 3. EIA (Environmental Impact Assessment)

**Definition:**
EIA is a systematic process to identify, predict, and evaluate the environmental effects of a proposed project *before* it starts. It decides if a project (like a dam, highway, factory) should be approved.

**Objective:**
*   Sustainable Development.
*   Prevent environmental degradation.
*   Public participation.

**Steps of EIA:** (Important for 7 Marks)

> **Mnemonic:** **S**creen **S**cope **B**ase **I**mpact **M**itigation **P**ublic **D**ecision **M**onitor
> (Screening, Scoping, Baseline Data, Impact Prediction, Mitigation, Public Hearing, Decision, Monitoring)

1.  **Screening:** Is EIA required? (Yes/No).
2.  **Scoping:** What are the key issues/boundaries?
3.  **Baseline Data Collection:** Current status of air, water, soil.
4.  **Impact Prediction:** Positive vs Negative impacts.
5.  **Mitigation Measures:** How to reduce negative impacts?
6.  **Public Hearing:** Asking local people for their opinion.
7.  **Decision Making:** Approval or Rejection by Govt (MoEFCC).
8.  **Post-Monitoring:** Checking compliance after construction.

## 4. Food Chain & Food Web

### Food Chain (Linear)
**Definition:** A linear sequence of organisms where nutrients and energy are transferred from one organism to another.
*   **Example:** Grass $\rightarrow$ Grasshopper $\rightarrow$ Frog $\rightarrow$ Snake $\rightarrow$ Eagle.
*   **Types:**
    1.  **Grazing Food Chain:** Starts with green plants (Sun is energy source).
    2.  **Detritus Food Chain:** Starts with dead organic matter (Decomposers).

### Food Web (Network)
**Definition:** A complex network of interconnected food chains. In nature, food chains are never isolated; they form a web.
*   **Significance:**
    *   Provides stability to the ecosystem.
    *   If one organism dies, the predator has other options (e.g., if Rabbit population drops, Lion eats Deer).
    *   Ensures flow of energy.

| Feature | Food Chain | Food Web |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Structure** | Single straight path | Complex network |
| **Stability** | Less stable (fragile) | More stable |
| **Realism** | Theoretical/Simple | Actual/Realistic |

**Types:**
1.  **Gaseous Cycles:** Reservoir is atmosphere involved (Nitrogen, Carbon).
2.  **Sedimentary Cycles:** Reservoir is earth crust (Phosphorus, Sulphur).

# UNIT 2: Natural Resources

## 1. Water Borne Disasters & Water Resources

**Water Resources:**
*   Only 3% of earth's water is fresh; 97% is salty (Oceans).
*   Sources: Surface water (Rivers, Lakes), Ground water (Wells).

**Water Borne Disasters:**
1.  **Floods:**
    *   **Causes:** Heavy rainfall, Dam failure, Deforestation (Soil can't hold water).
    *   **Effects:** Loss of life, damage to crops, spread of diseases (Cholera).
    *   **Management:** Afforestation, Dams/Levees, Flood warning systems.
2.  **Droughts:**
    *   **Causes:** Lack of rainfall, Over-exploitation of groundwater.
    *   **Effects:** Famine, Crop failure, Migration.
    *   **Management:** Rainwater Harvesting, Drip Irrigation.

## 2. Carbon, Nitrogen & Sulphur Cycles

### Nitrogen Cycle (Crucial for Proteins/DNA)
**Process:** Plants cannot take $N_2$ directly from air. It must be converted.
1.  **Nitrogen Fixation:** Converting $N_2 \rightarrow NH_3$ (Ammonia).
    *   *Natural:* Lightning.
    *   *Biological:* Bacteria (Rhizobium in legume roots).
2.  **Nitrification:** $NH_3 \rightarrow$ Nitrites ($NO_2^-$) $\rightarrow$ Nitrates ($NO_3^-$). (Plants absorb Nitrate).
3.  **Assimilation:** Plants use Nitrate to make proteins; Animals eat plants.
4.  **Ammonification:** Dead animals/plants $\rightarrow$ Ammonia (by decomposers).
5.  **Denitrification:** $NO_3^- \rightarrow N_2$ gas (back to atmosphere) by Pseudomonas bacteria.

### Carbon Cycle
*   **Photosynthesis:** Plants take $CO_2$ + Sunlight $\rightarrow$ Glucose + $O_2$.
*   **Respiration:** Animals take $O_2$ $\rightarrow$ Release $CO_2$.
*   **Decomposition/Combustion:** Burning fossil fuels releases stored carbon as $CO_2$.

## 3. Biogas Plant

**Definition:**
A plant that produces **Biogas** (Methane ~60% + $CO_2$) by anaerobic decomposition of organic waste (Cow dung, biomass).

**Construction:**
1.  **Mixing Tank:** Cow dung + Water = Slurry.
2.  **Digester Tank:** Sealed underground tank (No Oxygen). Bacteria ferment the slurry.
3.  **Gas Holder:** Dome where gas collects.
4.  **Outlet:** Removes "Sludge" (used as fertilizer).

**Advantages:**
*   Eco-friendly fuel.
*   Produces rich fertilizer (Sludge).
*   Cleanest burning fuel for villages.

# UNIT 3: Pollution

## 1. Solid Waste Management

**Definition:** Managing waste from its generation to final disposal.

**Functional Elements (The Process):**
1.  **Generation:** Household/Industrial refuse.
2.  **Storage:** Dustbins at source.
3.  **Collection:** Municipality trucks.
4.  **Transport:** To disposal site.
5.  **Disposal/Treatment:**
    *   **Open Dumping:** (Bad)
    *   **Sanitary Landfill:** Engineered method, waste buried, lined to prevent leaching.
    *   **Incineration:** Burning waste (Reduces volume, generates energy, but causes air pollution).
    *   **Composting:** Biodegradable waste $\rightarrow$ Manure.

**The 3R Principle:**
*   **Reduce:** Generate less waste.
*   **Reuse:** Use items again (e.g., glass bottles).
*   **Recycle:** Process waste into new material.

## 2. Case Studies (Must Learn)

### London Smog (1952) - "Classical Smog"
*   **Cause:** Burning of **Coal** (Sulphur Dioxide $SO_2$) + Fog in winter.
*   **Nature:** Reducing smog.
*   **Impact:** 4000+ deaths due to respiratory failure.

### Los Angeles Smog - "Photochemical Smog"
*   **Cause:** Automobile Exhaust ($NO_x$ + Hydrocarbons) + Sunlight.
*   **Prevention:** Catalytic converters in cars.
*   **Nature:** Oxidizing smog (Brown Haze).

### Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)
*   **Date:** Dec 2/3, 1984.
*   **Plant:** Union Carbide India Ltd (Pesticide plant).
*   **Gas Leaked:** **MIC (Methyl Isocyanate)**.
*   **Reason:** Water entered the MIC tank $\rightarrow$ High Pressure $\rightarrow$ Explosion.
*   **Impact:** Thousands died instantly, genetic defects continue today.

## 3. Noise Pollution

**Definition:** Unwanted or disturbing sound that affects health/wellbeing. Measured in **Decibels (dB)**. (safe limit: < 65 dB).

**Causes:**
*   Industrial machines.
*   Transport (Traffic, Jets).
*   Loudspeakers/Crackers.

**Effects:**
1.  **Auditory:** Temporary or Permanent Hearing Loss (Deafness).
2.  **Non-Auditory:** Hypertension (High BP), Stress, Insomnia, Loss of concentration.

## 4. Water Pollution & Minamata Disease

**Types:**
*   **Point Source:** Pipe discharge (Factory).
*   **Non-Point Source:** Agricultural runoff (hard to trace).

**Water Pollution Diseases:**
*   **Cholera/Typhoid:** Biological (Bacteria).
*   **Blue Baby Syndrome:** Nitrate pollution.
*   **Minamata Disease:**

# UNIT 4: Social Issues

## 1. Greenhouse Effect & Global Warming

**Greenhouse Effect (GHE):**
*   **Concept:** A natural process where atmospheric gases trap heat (Infrared radiation) from the earth, keeping it warm enough for life. Without it, Earth would be -18°C.
*   **Greenhouse Gases (GHGs):** $CO_2$ (Main), Methane ($CH_4$), $N_2O$, CFCs, Water Vapor.

**Global Warming:**
*   **Definition:** The **enhanced** greenhouse effect. Excess GHGs due to human activity (burning fossil fuels, deforestation) trap *too much* heat, raising Earth's average temperature.
*   **Effects:** Melting polar ice caps, Sea level rise (Coastal flooding), Extreme weather.

---

## 2. Acid Rain

**Definition:** Rain with pH < 5.6.

**Causes (Chemical Equations are vital for 7 Marks):**
1.  **Sulphur Dioxide ($SO_2$):** From coal industries.
    *   $SO_2 + H_2O \rightarrow H_2SO_4$ (Sulphuric Acid)
2.  **Nitrogen Oxides ($NO_x$):** From vehicle exhaust.
    *   $NO_x + H_2O \rightarrow HNO_3$ (Nitric Acid)

**Effects:**
*   **"Stone Cancer":** Corrodes marble (Taj Mahal turning yellow).
*   Kills aquatic life (Acidifies lakes).
*   Damages crops and soil fertility.

---

## 3. Ozone Layer Depletion

**Ozone Layer:** Located in Stratosphere. Acts as Earth's shield against UV-B rays.

**Cause of Depletion:**
*   **CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons):** From ACs, Refrigerators.
*   **Mechanism:** UV breaks CFC $\rightarrow$ Releases Chlorine (Cl). **One Cl atom can destroy 1,00,000 Ozone molecules.**

**Effects:**
*   **Humans:** Skin Cancer, Cataracts, Weak immunity.
*   **Environment:** Damages phytoplankton (affects aquatic food chain).
**Montreal Protocol (1987):** International treaty to ban CFCs to save Ozone.

# UNIT 5: Human Population & Environment

## 1. NGOs (Non-Governmental Organizations)

**Definition:**
Non-profit, voluntary citizens' groups that are organized on a local, national, or international level to address issues in support of the public good. They operate independently of the government.

**Role of NGOs in Environmental Protection:**
1.  **Creating Awareness:** Organizing rallies, street plays, and campaigns to educate people about pollution and conservation (e.g., Say No to Plastic).
2.  **Acting as a Watchdog:** Monitoring the activities of the government and industries to ensure they strictly follow environmental laws.
3.  **Public Interest Litigation (PIL):** Filing legal cases in courts against polluters or government inaction.
    *   *Example:* **M.C. Mehta** (Environmental Lawyer) filing cases for Taj Mahal protection and Ganga cleaning.
4.  **Research & Documentation:** Conducting studies on pollution levels and publishing reports (e.g., CSE's report on pesticides in cola).
5.  **Grassroots Action:** Tree plantation drives, rainwater harvesting projects, and waste management in villages.

**Important NGOs (Examples):**
*   **International:**
    *   **WWF (World Wide Fund for Nature):** Focuses on wildlife conservation (Giant Panda logo).
    *   **Greenpeace:** Focuses on climate change and anti-nuclear issues.
    *   **IUCN:** Publishes the 'Red Data Book' of endangered species.
*   **National (India):**
    *   **CSE (Centre for Science and Environment):** Led by Sunita Narain. Famous for Down To Earth magazine.
    *   **TERI (The Energy and Resources Institute):** Focuses on sustainable energy.
    *   **Chipko Movement:** Historic NGO-style movement led by Sunderlal Bahuguna.

## 2. Women Education & Environment

**"Educate a woman, you educate a nation."**

**Significance:**
1.  **Population Control:**
    *   There is a direct **inverse relationship** between Female Literacy and Total Fertility Rate (TFR).
    *   Educated women usually marry later and have fewer, healthier children.
    *   They are more aware of family planning methods.
2.  **Health & Hygiene:**
    *   Educated mothers understand the importance of sanitation, clean water, and nutrition, reducing *Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)*.
3.  **Resource Management:**
    *   In rural India, women are the primary collectors of water, fuel wood, and fodder. Education helps them manage these resources sustainably.
4.  **Economic Empowerment:**
    *   Financial independence reduces dependence on large families for old-age support.

**Government Schemes:**
*   **Beti Bachao, Beti Padhao:** To prevent female foeticide and ensure education.
*   **Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana:** Saving scheme for the girl child.

## 3. Human Population Growth & Explosion

**Population:**
*   **Definition:** A group of individuals of the same species living in a defined geographical area at a specific time, capable of interbreeding.

**Population Explosion:**
*   **Definition:** A sudden and massive increase in population size that exceeds the carrying capacity of the environment. It occurs when the birth rate (Natality) is much higher than the death rate (Mortality).
*   **Doubling Time:** The time taken for a population to double in size ($T_d = 70/growth\_rate$).

**Causes of Population Explosion:**
1.  **Falling Death Rates:** Better medical facilities and disease control have increased life expectancy.
2.  **High Birth Rates:** Due to illiteracy, early marriages, and lack of family planning awareness.
3.  **Social/Cultural Factors:** Preference for a male child ("Son preference") leads to larger families.
4.  **Poverty:** Children are seen as economic assets (earning hands) rather than liabilities.

**Effects of Population Explosion (The Problem):**
1.  **Resource Depletion:** Over-exploitation of essential resources like water, land, and fossil fuels.
2.  **Environmental Degradation:** Increased demand leads to deforestation, loss of biodiversity, and high pollution levels (Air/Water/Soil).
3.  **Unemployment & Poverty:** The economy cannot generate sufficient jobs for the rapidly growing workforce, leading to mass unemployment and slums.
4.  **Food Scarcity:** Demand outstrips supply, leading to inflation and food insecurity.
5.  **Health Issues:** Overcrowding aids the spread of infectious diseases and strains public health infrastructure.

**Variation Among Nations:**
*   **Developed Nations (e.g., Japan, Europe):** Negative or Zero growth (aging population issue).
*   **Developing Nations (e.g., India, Nigeria):** Rapid growth (population explosion phase).

**Control Measures:**
*   **Women Education:** Educated women have fewer children (Inverse relationship with fertility).
*   **Family Planning:** Promoting contraceptives and small family norms.
*   **Marriage Age:** Strict enforcement of legal age (18 for girls, 21 for boys).

## 4. Environment Protection Act (EPA), 1986

**Background:**
*   Enacted under **Article 253** of the Indian Constitution.
*   Triggered immediately after the **Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984)**.
*   Known as **"Umbrella Legislation"** because it covers existing laws (Water Act, Air Act) and fills gaps.

**Objectives:**
1.  To protect and improve the quality of the environment.
2.  To coordinate the activities of state governments and other authorities.
3.  To prevent, control, and abate environmental pollution.

**Key Powers of Central Government (Salient Features):**
1.  **Standard Setting:** Lay down standards for the quality of air, water, and soil.
2.  **Industrial Restrictions:** Decide areas where industries cannot be located (e.g., no polluting industries near Taj Mahal).
3.  **Inspection & Entry:** Officials can enter any factory to inspect equipment and take samples.
4.  **Closure Authority:** The Central Govt can order the **closure** of any industry or stoppage of electricity/water supply if it violates norms (Section 5).

**Penalties:**
*   Imprisonment up to **5 years**.
*   Fine up to **₹1 Lakh**.
*   Or Both.
*   If offense continues, fine of ₹5,000 per day.

## 5. Water & Air Acts (Salient Features)

### A. Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974
*   **Objective:** To prevent and control water pollution and maintain the wholesomeness of water.
*   **Salient Features:**
    1.  **Board Establishment:** Creation of **CPCB** (Central Pollution Control Board) and **SPCB** (State Boards).
    2.  **Powers:** Boards can take water samples from any stream/well for analysis.
    3.  **Prohibition:** Industries cannot discharge sewage/trade effluent into rivers without consent.
    4.  **Penalties:** Imprisonment from 1.5 to 6 years + Fine.

### B. Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981
*   **Objective:** To prevent, control, and abate air pollution.
*   **Salient Features:**
    1.  **Pollution Control Areas:** State Govt can declare any area as an "Air Pollution Control Area" (e.g., Delhi NCR).
    2.  **Vehicle Standards:** Empowers Boards to lay down exhaust standards for automobiles.
    3.  **Industrial Consent:** No person can establish an industrial plant in a pollution control area without SPCB consent.
    4.  **Penalties:** Imprisonment from 1.5 to 6 years + Fine.

## 6. Family Welfare Programme

**Definition:**
An integrated programme launched by Govt of India (first country in the world to launch it in 1952) to stabilize population and improve health.

**Components:**
1.  **Family Planning:** Promoting contraceptives (condoms, pills) and sterilization operations.
2.  **Maternal & Child Health (MCH):** Immunization, nutritional supplements (Iron/Folic acid) for pregnant women.
3.  **Rural Health Services:** Setting up Primary Health Centres (PHCs).

## 7. Master Question: Population & Environment
> **Note:** This single question covers all potential 7-mark questions related to population.

**Q. Define 'Population Explosion'. Discuss its causes and effects on the environment. Explain the role of the Family Welfare Programme and Women Education in controlling population growth.**

**Answer:**

**1. Definition:**
*   **Population Explosion:** A sudden and massive increase in population size that exceeds the carrying capacity of the region. It occurs when **Natality (Birth Rate)** significantly exceeds **Mortality (Death Rate)**.
*   **Doubling Time:** The time required for a population to double in size.

**2. Causes of Population Explosion:**
*   **Falling Death Rates:** Advancement in medical science (vaccines, antibiotics) has reduced mortality.
*   **High Birth Rates:** Persists due to lack of awareness.
*   **Social Factors:** Early marriages (<18 yrs), "Son Preference" (desire for a male child).
*   **Poverty:** Poor families view children as "earning hands" rather than liabilities.
*   **Illiteracy:** Lack of knowledge about family planning and contraceptives.

**3. Effects on Environment:**
*   **Resource Depletion:** Excessive pressure on water, land, and forests leading to scarcity.
*   **Pollution:** More people = More waste = Air, Water, and Soil pollution.
*   **Deforestation:** Forests are cleared for agriculture and housing (Urbanization).
*   **Global Warming:** Higher energy consumption leads to increased $CO_2$ emissions.
*   **Loss of Biodiversity:** Habitat destruction leads to extinction of species.

**4. Role of Family Welfare Programme (FWP):**
*   **Launched:** India was the first country to launch this in **1952**.
*   **Objective:** To stabilize population growth and improve health.
*   **Components:**
    *   **Sterilization:** Vasectomy (Males) and Tubectomy (Females).
    *   **Contraceptives:** Distribution of Condoms, Pills (Mala-D), and IUDs (Copper-T).
    *   **Maternal & Child Health (MCH):** Immunization and nutrition to reduce Infant Mortality Rate (IMR).

**5. Role of Women Education:**
*   **Inverse Relationship:** Higher literacy $\rightarrow$ Lower Total Fertility Rate (TFR).
*   **Awareness:** Educated women delay marriage and understand the benefits of a small family.
*   **Empowerment:** Financial independence reduces reliance on children for old-age support.
*   **Decision Making:** Empowered women have a greater say in family planning.

**Conclusion:**
Population explosion is the root cause of environmental degradation. An integrated approach involving education, women empowerment, and effectively implemented family welfare programmes is essential for sustainable development.
